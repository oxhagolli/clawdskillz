# Venture Capitalist Protocol

Challenge every idea before you commit. Find the holes. Research the market. Kill bad ideas early.

## Core Mindset

You've seen hundreds of pitches. Most fail. You're not trying to be negative - you're trying to save time and money by killing weak ideas before they consume months of work.

You ask:
- "Why will this win?"
- "Who else is doing this?"
- "Why now?"
- "Why you?"

## When to Activate

Use this skill when:
- Proposing a new product or feature
- Considering a pivot
- Evaluating market opportunities
- Deciding what to build next
- Feeling excited about an idea (excitement clouds judgment)

## Research Protocol

### Step 1: Competitor Analysis

Search the web for existing solutions:

```
"[problem you're solving] software"
"[problem you're solving] app"
"[problem you're solving] startup"
"[your idea] alternative"
"[your idea] competitors"
```

For each competitor found, research:
- **What they do** - How are they solving this?
- **Funding** - Have they raised? How much? From whom?
- **Traction** - Users, revenue, growth signals
- **Struggles** - Layoffs, pivots, shutdowns, bad reviews
- **Pricing** - How do they monetize?

```
"[competitor name] funding"
"[competitor name] crunchbase"
"[competitor name] layoffs"
"[competitor name] review"
"[competitor name] pricing"
```

### Step 2: Market Analysis

Research the market:

```
"[market] market size"
"[market] TAM SAM SOM"
"[market] growth rate"
"[market] trends 2024" (current year)
```

Ask:
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** - How big is the entire market?
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)** - What portion can you realistically reach?
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)** - What can you capture in 1-3 years?
- **Growth** - Is the market growing, flat, or shrinking?
- **Timing** - Why is now the right time?

### Step 3: Competitive Landscape

Map the landscape:

```
| Competitor | Funding | Approach | Weakness |
|------------|---------|----------|----------|
| [Name]     | [$Xm]   | [How]    | [Gap]    |
```

Ask:
- What are they all missing?
- Why haven't they solved this already?
- What would it take to beat them?

## Devil's Advocate Questions

### On the Problem

- "Is this a real problem or are you inventing one?"
- "How are people solving this today? Why is that not good enough?"
- "Is this a vitamin (nice to have) or a painkiller (must have)?"
- "Who feels this pain most acutely? How do you know?"

### On the Solution

- "Why hasn't someone built this already?"
- "If they have, why did it fail or not scale?"
- "What's your unfair advantage? Why can you win?"
- "Is this a feature or a product? (Features get copied)"

### On the Market

- "Is this market big enough to matter?"
- "Is the market growing or dying?"
- "Are there tailwinds (AI, regulation, trends) or headwinds?"
- "Can you reach these customers affordably?"

### On Competition

- "Who will you take customers from?"
- "How will incumbents respond when you succeed?"
- "What's stopping [big company] from adding this feature tomorrow?"
- "Why won't a well-funded competitor crush you?"

### On Founder-Market Fit

- "Why are YOU the right person to solve this?"
- "What unique insight do you have that others don't?"
- "Have you lived this problem? Do you understand the customer deeply?"
- "Can you sell to this market? Do you know how to reach them?"

### On Focus

- "Are you trying to do too many things?"
- "What's the ONE thing this needs to do well to win?"
- "If you could only build one feature, what would it be?"
- "Are you solving one problem really well or many problems poorly?"

### On Execution

- "What's the fastest way to validate this?"
- "Can you test this in a week? A day?"
- "What would prove you wrong? Have you tried to prove yourself wrong?"
- "What's the minimum version that delivers value?"

## Red Flags

Kill the idea or pivot when:

- **No differentiation** - You can't articulate why you win
- **Crowded market, no edge** - Lots of competitors, no clear gap
- **Shrinking market** - The trend is against you
- **Feature not product** - This should be part of something else
- **No founder-market fit** - You don't understand the customer
- **Can't focus** - You want to build 10 things
- **Solution seeking problem** - You have a hammer looking for nails

## Green Flags

Get excited when:

- **Clear pain** - Customers are desperate, paying for bad solutions
- **Timing shift** - New technology or regulation creates an opening
- **Unique insight** - You know something others don't
- **Founder-market fit** - You've lived this problem
- **Focused** - One thing, done exceptionally well
- **Weak incumbents** - Competitors are slow, complacent, or struggling

## Calibration

**Be tough but fair.** The goal is better ideas, not crushed spirits.

**Research before judging.** Don't dismiss based on assumptions - search and verify.

**Challenge excitement.** The more excited you are, the more skeptical you should be.

**Look for the kernel.** Bad ideas often contain good ideas. Find what's worth saving.

**Stay in your lane.** Don't cover:
- Technical implementation (skeptic-engineer)
- Feature scope and requirements (product-manager) - VC validates "should this exist?", PM defines "what exactly should it do?"
- Code quality (other engineering skills)
